# The Sunday Service — Product North Star

> *Somewhere between oil and obsession.*

---

## What This Is

The Sunday Service is a premium car maintenance and service record app for iOS, built for the people who actually do the work — or at least care enough to document it properly. It is a tool for enthusiasts, not a reminder app for people who forget to change their oil.

The app exists because every serious car person eventually accumulates the same pile of problems: a maintenance notebook that lives in the glovebox, a folder of receipts that may or may not be complete, a mental model of what the car needs held together by memory and ritual. When it's time to sell, that pile is worth something — or it isn't. When it's time to wrench, that pile is either useful or it isn't. The Sunday Service turns that pile into a living record.

**The tagline is a promise:** *Your car remembers everything. Now so do you.*

---

## Brand

**The garage is open.**

The Sunday Service sits at the intersection of craft and obsession. It is not a wellness app. It is not a productivity tool. It is not trying to gamify your maintenance intervals. It is a serious piece of software for people who check the torque twice, who have opinions about fluid weights, who know that "close enough" is how you end up on the side of the road.

The four brand pillars:

**Obsession** — This app is for the person who knows the exact mileage of their last oil change, who tracks break-in intervals, who has a spreadsheet somewhere that the app will make obsolete. We are not trying to make car maintenance casual. We are building for the person who already treats it like a ritual.

**Precision** — Socket sizes. Torque specs. Part numbers. Fluid capacities to the half-quart. The app stores the exact recipe so you never have to reconstruct it from memory. Precision is not pedantry — it is respect for the machine.

**Ritual** — The Sunday morning oil change. The pre-season coolant flush. The post-track-day inspection. Some cars are maintained on a schedule; others are maintained as an act of care. The app honors both — dual triggers, intensity sessions, procedure templates you can run the same way every time.

**Memory** — Provenance is value. A complete, documented service history with photos, part numbers, and notes is worth real money when it's time to sell. More than that, it's a record of stewardship — proof that someone cared. The Dossier is the culmination of the memory pillar.

## The Problem with Every Other App

Most car maintenance apps are glorified reminder apps. Log a date. Log a mileage. Set a reminder. Done. They are useful in the way a Post-it note is useful — which is to say, barely.

The existing landscape:

- **Fuelly / Drivvo / Simply Auto** — mileage trackers with reminder functionality bolted on. Fine for the person who wants to know their MPG trend. Not useful for anyone doing actual maintenance work.
- **CARFAX Car Care** — designed to funnel you toward dealer service. The incentives are misaligned with the enthusiast.
- **GarageHub (2025)** — the closest competitor. Has parts inventory and an AI chatbot called "Rev." But it launched chatbot-first, gates core features behind a subscription, and has no community, no culture, and no point of view. The chatbot-as-hero philosophy ages poorly. Nobody would wear a GarageHub t-shirt.

The gap is obvious once you look for it: there is no app built from the perspective of someone who actually works on their own car. The Sunday Service is the first one.

## Product Philosophy

**The car is the hero.**

The app opens to your car — not a chat prompt, not a dashboard, not a list of upcoming reminders. Your car. Its profile, its history, its current state. Everything flows from that.

AI is the craftsman's assistant, not the product. It pre-fills procedure templates with torque specs and fluid capacities. It detects anomalies in your service patterns. It writes the narrative for your Dossier export. It does its job quietly, in the background, and then gets out of the way.

The moat is not the AI model — any competitor can swap in a better model tomorrow. The moat is the user's data: years of service records, part numbers, photos, torque notes, and lessons learned that live in the app and nowhere else. That data becomes more valuable every year. When it's time to sell the car, that data is a differentiator. When it's time to service the car, that data is a tool.

**The feature loop:**

```
Procurement → Execution → Documentation → Asset Value
(Parts Shelf)   (Garage Mode + Templates)   (Visual Ledger)   (The Dossier)
```

Every feature in the app lives somewhere in that loop. Features that don't serve the loop don't ship.

## Core Differentiators

### 1. Procedure Templates

Reusable service procedures that capture everything you need before you start: socket sizes, fluid capacities, torque specs, step-by-step notes, required tools. Tap "Run Procedure" and the full job brief is on screen.

Templates are built once and run every time. The first oil change takes ten minutes to document; every subsequent one takes thirty seconds to initiate and produces a complete record. Templates can be created from scratch, pre-filled by AI given a description of the job, or eventually shared by the community.

This is the feature that separates the app from every reminder-based competitor. A reminder tells you when to do the job. A procedure template tells you *how* to do the job, with your car's specific details already filled in.

### 2. Spec & Source Vault

The exact recipe for every service, preserved permanently. Fluid weights, viscosity grades, part numbers, source URLs, pricing at time of purchase. Not "oil change" — "Motul 8100 X-cess 5W-40, 10.5 quarts, Pelican Parts #MOT-102049, $89.50."

Reorder in one tap next season. Never reconstruct a parts list from memory or a three-year-old receipt again.

### 3. Parts Shelf

A physical inventory of what's actually in the garage. The app knows what the procedure requires, knows what you have on the shelf, and tells you exactly what to order before you start. "You need 10.5 quarts. You have 1.5 on the shelf. Order 9."

This closes the loop between planning and procurement — the feature that prevents the Sunday morning discovery that you're two quarts short.

### 4. Torque Specs & Lessons Learned

Factory torque specifications for every fastener, paired with personal notes the service manual doesn't include. "Needs 6-inch wobble extension to clear the intake." "Watch the wire harness — it sits right against the filter housing." "The pan drain plug is 13mm on this year, not 15mm."

These notes are the accumulated knowledge of every person who has worked on this car. They live with the procedure, not in a separate notebook, not in memory.

### 5. Visual Ledger

Photos attached to every service event. Dashboard mileage at service start. Empty fluid containers. Before-and-after of the drain plug. Receipts. Condition notes. Any detail that should be documented gets documented.

A service record without photos is a claim. A service record with photos is evidence. The Visual Ledger turns every entry into verified history — which matters at point of sale, in warranty disputes, and for any subsequent owner or inspector.

### 6. The Dossier Export

One-tap export of complete car history as a polished PDF or shareable web link. The AI synthesizes the raw data into a coherent ownership narrative: when the car was acquired, what work was done under this ownership, what condition it's in, what's coming up. Written in plain English, formatted professionally.

The Dossier is the payoff for every piece of data entered into the app. It justifies a premium asking price when selling. It communicates stewardship to a prospective buyer more effectively than any verbal claim. For a collector car, it is the difference between a transaction and an inheritance.

### 7. Cross-Reference Part Numbers

OEM part numbers alongside aftermarket and alternative part numbers, with sourcing options. The Ferrari oil filter housing sensor is a Bosch part — the same one, different box, at 30% of the price. The app knows this, or can find it, and stores it with the part record.

This feature respects the user's intelligence. The people using this app are already doing this research manually. The app just makes it systematic.

### 8. Garage Mode UI

A dedicated UI mode for active service — massive text, high-contrast dark theme, tap targets large enough to hit with a glove on. Readable from a workbench three feet away with dirty hands. The procedure checklist, torque values, and step notes are all visible without squinting at a normal-sized screen.

Most maintenance app interfaces are designed for the couch. Garage Mode is designed for the garage floor. That distinction is the difference between an app you check before you start and an app that's useful while you're working.

### 9. Dual-Trigger Intervals + Intensity Decay

Service intervals triggered by both mileage and elapsed time — whichever comes first. Standard behavior for any serious maintenance schedule.

Intensity Sessions add a third dimension: high-stress driving events (track days, canyon runs, timed rallies) that accelerate maintenance schedules based on the severity of the session. An engine that ran at sustained high RPMs for three hours needs to be evaluated on different terms than an engine that covered the same mileage in highway commuting. The app tracks the difference.

### 10. AI Procedure Intelligence

Describe a job in plain language and the AI pre-fills the procedure template: torque specs for that year/make/model, fluid capacities, common failure points, recommended tools. A starting point, not a finished product — the user reviews and refines, but starts from something rather than nothing.

AI does the research. The user does the judgment. The result is a procedure template that would have taken an hour to build from spec sheets and forum threads, completed in two minutes.

### 11. AI Anomaly Detection

Passive pattern recognition running against the user's service history. Not a chat interface — a quiet alert when something in the data doesn't match the expected pattern. Oil darkening faster than the previous three changes. Coolant consumption trending upward over six months. Brake fluid moisture creeping toward service threshold.

The user doesn't ask for this. The app notices it. That is the correct role for AI in a tool like this: a second set of eyes on the data, not a conversational interface that requires prompting.

## User Personas

**The Weekend Wrench** does all his own work. He has the factory service manual, two floor jacks, and opinions about impact wrenches. He's been keeping a maintenance log in a composition notebook for eight years. The Sunday Service replaces the notebook, adds photos and part numbers, and eventually produces a Dossier that makes the car easier to sell — or more valuable to keep.

**The Project Car Builder** is in the middle of a restoration or modification build. He needs parts tracking, a build plan, procedure documentation for non-standard work, and a way to capture the work-in-progress state of the car. The Parts Shelf and Spec & Source Vault are built for him. The Dossier tells the story of the build.

**The Collector/Custodian** owns cars that appreciate. Provenance is not a nice-to-have — it is a financial consideration. He may not do his own work, but he documents every service, every part, every specialist who touched the car. The Visual Ledger and Dossier are the core value proposition. He may not use the torque specs feature; he will absolutely use the export feature.

**The Track Day Driver** runs his car hard and tracks the consequences. Intensity Sessions are built for him. His maintenance intervals are not the same as the manual's — they reflect actual duty cycle. He needs anomaly detection more than most, because he is more likely to notice early signs of stress if they're surfaced.

## Monetization

**One-time purchase with optional Pro unlock.**

This is not a subscription app. The subscription model is correct for software that provides ongoing service delivery — cloud sync, live data feeds, continuous feature development funded by recurring revenue. For a tool you own, in a category where trust is everything, the subscription model is a misalignment between the product's value proposition and its business model.

You buy a torque wrench once. You buy this app once.

Pro unlocks: AI features (procedure intelligence, anomaly detection, Dossier AI narrative), unlimited vehicle profiles, Dossier PDF and web exports, advanced cross-referencing. Core functionality — service log, photo documentation, reminders, procedure templates, Parts Shelf — available at base price.

This fits the brand. Tools you own, not rent. The user's data is theirs; the app doesn't hold it hostage to a monthly fee.

## Platform Strategy

**iOS first.** The audience skews Apple. The quality bar for the UI — particularly Garage Mode and the Dossier export — is easier to achieve and maintain on a single platform. Build it right on one platform before expanding.

**Landing page now.** Brand presence, product positioning, waitlist capture. The tone of the landing page sets expectations before the app ships. The landing page is the first impression, and the first impression is the brand.

**Merch.** This is a culture play, not a revenue strategy. Limited run t-shirts, hats, stickers. "The garage is open." The kind of thing someone actually wears to a Cars and Coffee because it's not embarrassing. Merch signals that this is a brand people want to be associated with, not just a utility they use. That signal matters for word-of-mouth in a community where word-of-mouth is everything.

**Web app later.** Dossier viewing, service history browsing, some data entry. Not a replacement for the mobile experience — a complement to it.

**Android later.** After the iOS product is right.

## Roadmap

### Phase 1 — MVP

The minimum viable experience: you can add a car, log services with photos, set reminders that trigger on mileage and time, and build procedure templates.

- Vehicle profile (year, make, model, current mileage, acquisition details)
- Service log with photo attachment
- Dual-trigger reminders (mileage + time)
- Procedure templates (manual creation)
- Basic Spec & Source Vault entry

**The test:** Can the Weekend Wrench replace his composition notebook with this app?

### Phase 2 — Depth

The features that make the app irreplaceable for serious users.

- Parts Shelf with inventory tracking and procurement alerts
- Full Spec & Source Vault with one-tap reorder links
- Cross-reference part numbers
- Lessons Learned notes on procedures
- Garage Mode UI
- Torque spec storage per fastener

**The test:** Can the Project Car Builder track an entire engine rebuild in this app?

### Phase 3 — Intelligence

AI features that are only possible because the data foundation is already there.

- AI procedure pre-fill (describe the job, get the template)
- AI anomaly detection (passive alerts from service history patterns)
- Dossier PDF export with AI-written narrative
- Secure shareable Dossier web link
- Intensity Sessions with maintenance schedule decay

**The test:** Does the Collector/Custodian use the Dossier to justify a higher asking price?

### Phase 4 — Platform

Expansion after the core product is proven.

- Merch store (limited runs, brand-first)
- Web app (Dossier viewing, service history)
- Procedure template sharing (community library)
- Android

**The test:** Do people wear the hat?

## What This Is Not

The Sunday Service is not trying to be everything to everyone. It is not a fuel economy tracker. It is not a navigation app. It is not a dealership service scheduler. It is not a social network for car people. It is not a marketplace.

It is a record-keeping and execution tool for people who take their cars seriously. It does that job better than anything else, and it does not apologize for being narrow.

Narrow and deep beats broad and shallow. Every time.
